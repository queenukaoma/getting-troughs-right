The Problem

Unreliable trough data leads to unsafe dosing decisions.

Heart transplant recipients depend on precise immunosuppressant levels. When troughs aren't drawn in the correct window or last-dose times aren't documented, the lab value is misleading — leading to unnecessary dose changes, repeat draws, and delayed decisions.

Project Aim

Standardize when and how troughs are collected.

Implement an NP-led standardized trough collection protocol with last-dose documentation to improve the reliability and timeliness of immunosuppressant levels used for dosing decisions in adult heart transplant inpatients.

Who Qualifies?

Inclusion & Exclusion Criteria

Every shift, ask: does this patient qualify for the trough timing protocol?

Include
✅ Adult (age ≥ 18)
✅ Heart transplant recipient (OHT)
✅ Admitted to ICU, IMC, or Telemetry
✅ On tacrolimus, sirolimus, cyclosporine, or everolimus
✅ Trough level ordered today
Exclude
❌ OR day or code event (timing not feasible)
❌ Clinically unstable / NPO / pre-procedural
❌ Transferred off participating unit mid-draw
❌ Both actual and planned dose times missing

The Intervention

Standardized Trough Collection Protocol

A simple, fixed workflow that removes variability from trough collection.

By 07:30
Check: is today's trough order present? If not, page the transplant provider.
08:15 – 08:45
DRAW FIRST. Collect the trough level before administering the morning dose. Target time: 08:30. This is the C0 window — 0 to 60 minutes before the next dose.
By 09:00
THEN DOSE. Administer the scheduled immunosuppressant dose immediately after the draw. Do not hold the dose for the result.
MAR Documentation
Record the actual administration clock time for every dose (morning and evening). If a dose is given late (>30 min off schedule), document the reason in the MAR note — procedure, NPO, pt refused, transfer, medication delay, etc. This context is essential for interpreting the next trough.
Escalation
Contact the transplant provider immediately if: the 08:30 window is missed (it's past 09:00 with no draw); night shift drew with 01:00–06:00 labs; the dose was given BEFORE the trough was drawn (the level is invalid as a C0); or there is any dosing ambiguity.
🚫
Do NOT draw troughs with routine 01:00–06:00 AM labs
⏲️
Do NOT hold the dose waiting for the lab result
⛔
If the dose was given BEFORE the draw — the trough is invalid. Contact the transplant provider.

What The Data Revealed

Six signals that shaped the protocol.

Every number below is drawn from the locked 8-week baseline (N=163 analytic draws, 14 patients). Each card is a specific, quantified finding — and a direct input into how the intervention was designed.

11
Overnight Draws
Eleven draws landed between 01:00–05:59 with routine overnight labs. These are not C0 troughs — they're 3–8 hours too early. Every single one distorted the clinical picture.
100%
Repeat Draws = Early-AM
Every single repeat draw (7/7) originated from an early-AM mistiming. Eliminate the overnight draw, eliminate the waste. One root cause, one intervention point.
95.7%
Documentation is Solid
Last-dose times are documented in the MAR 95.7% of the time. Nurses are already engaged — the gap is timing, not culture. The protocol reinforces an existing strength.
36.2%
Only 1 in 3 Hit Target
Just 36.2% of draws land in the 08:15–08:45 window. That is the opportunity. A fixed window plus visual cues plus audit-and-feedback is how this number climbs past 70%.
IMC
52% on One Unit
52% of all baseline draws occurred on IMC (85 of 163). The rollout emphasizes IMC first — training here moves most of the care, fastest.
6
Admission Disruption
Six draws happened in the context of a new admission or readmission, where logistics disrupt timing. The protocol adds a "first 24 hours" layer specifically for newly admitted transplant recipients.
